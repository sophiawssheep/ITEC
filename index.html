Living in private domestic flats

The latest available government statistics showed that Hong Kong's private residential property prices had spiked since 2010, there was a double increase from last year. And the rental index, had also gone up steadily. According to Demographia International Housing Affordability Survey, Hong Kong's private home prices increased by 490 per cent since 2013, with those for the smaller flats scaled up by 573 per cent. This situation gives it the renowned distinction of being the world's most expensive real estate market.



Despite of unstoppable rise in flat price, the salary growth far less than the increment scale of private domestic residential price rise.

With reference to the 14th Annual Demographia International Housing Affordability Survey carried by urban planning policy consultancy Demographia in 2017, which covers 293 cities in nine countries, e.g. China, the United States, Singapore, etc. Hong Kong is the world’s most expensive city to live in as disparity between the median home price and the median household income continues to expand.

 

The result unveiled on Monday showed Hong Kong topped the list for the eighth year in a row, with home prices regarded by Demographia as being “least affordable”. And it was the only Chinese city to be ranked in the list.The figure was up from 18.1 times a year earlier and the highest level ever recorded since the survey began in 2005.

The median multiple, or the ratio of the median price of property to the median household income, was 19.4 times in Hong Kong,according to the survey. That means it will take 19.4 years for an average household to afford an apartment in Hong Kong even if there were no other expenses, e.g. no consumption of food and shelter.

Living in public rental housing

As at end-September 2018, there were about 150,200 general applications for PRH, and about 117,500 non-elderly one-person applications under the Quota and Points System. The average waiting time for general applicants was 5.5 years. Among them, the average waiting time for elderly one-person applicants was 2.9 years.




Nowadays, it is argued that the income thresold for PRH applicants is not aligned to the market salary level. According to the statistics released in 2018, the median monthly wage of employees in Hong Kong was $16,800 in May-Jun 2017. For a 2-people household, if both of the memebrs are working class and having around $16,800 as monthly income, in addition to basic investment, they would exceed the income and asset limit set by the government. While having this income level could not allow residents to make a life in Hong Kong easily, due to the high price index of the city. Some citizens may need to change their job or voluntarily cut down their income in order to pass the income and asset limit of PRH application, which even makes their life harder.

Living in Subdivided Units

There are many people in Hong Kong living in subdivided units with little room to live.

Cage House

Subdivided unit is also called as cage house or bedspace apartments homes.

In one room, usually occupies 5-6 people with the size of one cage (2 × 0,5 meters).

Expensive Cage House

The price for cage house is high.

Even though the size for cage house is really small, one can be rented for HK $ 1,500-2,000 (US$200- US$255).

Living Conditions

The safety and other living conditions in these cage house are often poor.

The living conditions in subdivided flats , including hygiene, facilities,etc are poor. For example, there's rats running in the flats at night.

When people cannot afford private domestic flats, and they are not entitled to public residential housing, they can only rely on subdivided units to gain a shelter. According to the "2016 Population By-census Thematic Report: Persons Living in Subdivided Units", There were around 200 thousand people live in approximately 92 thousand subdivided units (SDU), with an average of 2.3 people per household. This number broke the data of past years record.The median of subdivided flats area were 5.3 meter square, lower than that of normal Hong Kong household flats. The research revealed that there were a large number of children and youth made up the composition of residents.









Overall speaking, there were more children and young to middle-aged persons living in SDUs. With most of the residents are married and most of the residents did not receive tertiary education. In addition, most of the residents had monthly household income of below $15,000. There is another shocking result, the report revealed that there were more than 5000 SDU residents were managerial or above excutives and professional practitioners, whom traditionally perceived as the midde-class members in Hong Kong.

It is observed that the young people would like to live separately from their family, while Hong Kong's property price is unaffordable to them. SDUs become popular among the young people and professional individuals, who would like to enjoy an individual resting area. Nonetheless, SDU owners tend to raise rents continuously and expensively charge the residents for utitilies usage. This creates a high burden on low-income individuals.



The geographical distribution of households living in SDUs was similar to that of SDUs. 56.9% of persons living in SDUs were in Kowloon, concentrating in the Yau Tsim Mong district (22.5%), Sham Shui Po district (16.7%) and Kowloon City district (10.0%). These were also the top 3 districts in terms of households living in SDUs.

Reasons for housing problem in HK

Shortage of Land

Today Hong Kong's population is estimated to be 7.43 million, which makes it one of the most densely populated areas in the world. Hong Kong has a population density of 6,300 people per square kilometer. However, we just have a land mass of 1,104 square kilometers with a highly developed transportation network and densely populated buildings.

Property Speculation

People buy the house in HK not only because they have the needs for living, but also for speculation. They expected the housing price will keep constant or even increase in the future, which also increase the demand for housing.

Abuse of Land Rights

The past Chief Executive Leung Chun-ying had once mentioned that Hong Kong lacks the land that is developable. There is severe abuse of land rights in the New Territories due to the failure in managing "Ding" rights. Some of the originally agricultural land were converted by landowner into a “brownfield site” for car parks or other commercial use, making huge profits as a result. It is clearly unaffordable for Hong Kong when land is so scarce.

Expensive Land

Even though government is trying to impose polices on lowering the housing price, the government was practicing a ‘high land price’ policy to bid the land in order to maintain high land sale revenue. If the bidding price is lower than government's expectation, the government will take back the land. The developers need to buy the land in a high price, which also has led to high home prices.



Conclusion

The scarcity of affordable housing in Hong Kong has been rooted deeply and it got worsen these years. It's not easy to solve, it should be the top priority for the HKSAR Government.
